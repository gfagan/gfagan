Field Notes

Note: The writeup for this section is still in progress

Background

Data recording in the field, particularly in Archaeology and Cultural Heritage Management, still tends to be done using paper record sheets. Numerous attempts have been made to ‘digitise’ the recording process, but the efforts so far have either been unwieldy, expensive, or simply a digital version of a paper recording sheet.

Phoenix Heritage, our Industry Partner, proposed a Smartphone/Tablet-based recording system, linked to a database in the cloud or on a local server, and driven by onscreen prompts for more efficient data gathering and recording.

Phoenix Heritage is a technology consultancy involved in the development of innovative hardware devices and software applications for the Heritage Industry.

Some Context

For our Final Project, we were assigned into groups and given a project proposal from one of Digital Skills Academy’s Industry partners.

Our Industry Partner (IP) was Phoenix Heritage, a technology consultancy involved in the development of innovative hardware devices and software applications for the Heritage Industry

The Project Proposal

Data recording in the field, particularly in Archaeology and Cultural Heritage Management, still tends to be done using paper record sheets. Numerous attempts have been made to ‘digitise’ the recording process, but the efforts so far have either been unwieldy, expensive, or simply a digital version of a paper recording sheet.

Some excavations have 20000+ pieces of record that are fragmented, requiring recruitment of resources to manually input, and overall can be time-consuming, costly and have duplication of effort.

Field Notes is a solution being developed by Phoenix Heritage in response to that. They are looking to digitise the laborious manual record gathering and management process involved in a standard archaeological dig, which to-date still involve only paper record sheets. Phoenix Heritage looked to Team Azteca to gather enough material to provide a product proof of concept for Field Notes, that could be presented to potential investors.

My Role

We had a team of five committed to the application of Agile Methodologies, for which I was the UX Designer. My responsibility was to focus primarily on the user research and prototyping aspect of the project.

The other project roles were as follows:

  • Project Manager
  • Scrum Master
  • Marketing & Business Analyst
  • Developer

From Phoenix Heritage we also had Stuart, the company's founder, who acted as their representative thorughout the process. Stuart is an experienced and licensed Senior Archaeologist with over 30 years of professional consultancy experience.

Identifying Early Challenges

At our first team meeting, we put together a team charter and identified some non-technical challenges that we would need to take into account.

  1. Our project team was split between two time zones, with three members being in Ireland and the other two living in Dubai.
  2. All team members had a full time job.
  3. A relatively quick turnaround in terms of the project timelines was required, especially considering we also had multiple video presentation assessments to be produced in conjunction with deliverables for the Industry Partner.
  4. Getting time with other archaeologists and potential app users outside of Stuart would be incredibly difficult considering the circumstances.

To offset having a globally set team, we relied heavily on digital applications, such as Whatsapp, Trello, and Google Hangouts, Drive and Calendar.

With time restrictions in mind, we set ourselves a weekly time slot for our team meeting, and a bi-weekly slot for a catch-up with our Industry Partner.

Setting the Scope

After the team was established and we had received the project proposal, we arranged a meeting with Stuart to carry out a deep dive of the initial brief to begin identifying the project’s deliverables, determining what was possible with respect to the time, cost and quality available to us.

Following this first meeting with Stuart, I arranged a separate meeting in order to carry out some research and define the user requirements. This meeting between helped lay the foundation for our personas, as well as outlining some key items for consideration, such as the poor lighting conditions and potential lack of phone data reception that archaeologists may be faced with when on site.

It was from this discussion that we settled on there being no requirement for company branding, or a high-fidelity prototype and accompanying style guide. Instead, we agreed on the delivery of a high level information architecture, wireframes, and a low-fidelity prototype.

Following this, we produced a scope document outlining the project at hand, the scope of work and what was out of scope, as well as the projected timelines, tasks and costs.

To summarise, we set ourselves the following deliverables to be achieved over four two-week sprint cycles:

  1. A Marketing Plan, to include a marketing strategy and plan, as well as a competitor analysis.
  2. A UX Design, following initial user research into personas and scenarios and resolting in an information architecture diagram.
  3. A UI Design in the form of wireframes of the office admin user's dashboard, and wireframes and a low fidelity prototype of the archaeologist user's app view.
  4. A Technology Roadmap, including a backend architecture diagram, some cloud storage solution comparisons, and a product roadmap.
  5. Some Usability Testing on potential end-users.
  6. A Project Closure Report document, to formalise the closure of the project, outlining the criteria for deliverable acceptance.

Project Journey - Sprint 0

The main methodology used for this project was agile. The team’s scrum master worked on the timelines and tasks required to produce the deliverables. Armed with clear deliverables, we managed our work using Agile, and broke the timeline down into four 2 weeks Sprints.

We documented each of our Sprints in terms of plans, reviews and retrospectives and these were shared with our Industry Partner at the start, midway point, and end of each sprint. This helped ensure transparency and maintain alignment of direction.

As archaeology was a new area of focus for the entire team, we were reliant on Stuart to give us background on the need for the solution he was looking to develop. Our Scrum Master had experience as a digital product owner in her professional life, and she used these insights to develop a product canvas based on both Stuart’s knowledge and the findings of the team. This was a very powerful tool that helped us to identify the key elements of the application.

Our marketing analyst carried out competitor research, investigating other software or applications already in the market with any semblance to our project. This search gave us some valuable insights and armed with these, we were able to bring these to Stuart and work out in greater detail what success looks like for his solution.

With all this information we started to developed a Marketing Plan that will help Phoenix Heritage to create awareness and commercialise. Within the Marketing Plan is included the the competitor analysis and also the designing of a target audience, 3 personas with their user stories, marketing objectives, channels, KPI per channel, measurement tools and a strategy summary with all the info needed to developed the plan. The Marketing plan was a work in progress during Sprint 0, 1 and 2. Working directly with Stuart and his feedback along the way.

My focus during Sprint 0 was the UX Requirements, which I’ve already discussed. Further to those discussions with Stuart, I also read up on best practices around user input and form design as I knew that this would be a key aspect of the Field Notes application.

Sprint 1 - Technology Roadmap

In this sprint, our Developer began putting together a technology roadmap document. Here, she laid out the back-end app architecture, as well as the languages recommended to be used for each section of the software's development.

It also provided Phoenix Heritage with a breakdown of comparisons of cloud storage solution options, as well as our recommendations for the best road forward for them in terms of data storage architecture. Inclusive in the document was a Product Roadmap that detailed the next 12 months of their development of the Field Notes software, in a timeline style diagram, as shown below.

Work continued on the technology roadmap document into sprint 2, building out on sections the IP indicated he would like more detail on.

Sprint 1 - Information Architecture

For this Sprint, I began to direct questions at Stuart in relation to expected content and functionality in order to determine the key screens for Field Notes. Ultimately, Field Notes is a data input application relative to Archaeology Excavations, and the context sheets and record sheets that are produced from these.

The first draft of the Information Architecture, as displayed below, combined functionality for both the Regular User and the Admin User. After presenting this to Stuart and getting his feedback, I split this into two separate architectures and we decided to provide Admin functionality through Desktop only.

Above you can see the Mobile application, which contains only functionality for the regular user. On the right, we have the Desktop Information Architecture, and it is here where the Admin functionality comes to life.

The complete separation of the Admin functionality from the Fields Notes application gives Stuart exactly what he was looking, something that is simple, straightforward, and easy to use.

Sprint 2 - Wireframes

With the Information Architecture settled upon, I started mocking up Wireframes using InVision Freehand. This allowed me to rapidly produce the displayed low-fidelity screens, receive feedback, and make amendments with ease.

With our Personas in mind, I knew that we had to create an application that was both intuitive and informative to the users, catering for the young tech savvy Archaeologist who is a little unsure of the recording process, and the elder experienced Archaeologist who is a little uncomfortable with technology.

With Stuarts direction and feedback, this let to the single input system that you can see here.

I coupled this with contextual help for every field, and also a progress bar that details where along the input process the user is.

The Wireframes for the Desktop Admin screens were also produced using InVision Freehand. Again, having the Information Architecture complete at this point was a massive help, as it allowed me to quickly think of the best way of structuring the content in a manner that both allows for a great user experience, but also is future proofed for Stuart and Phoenix heritage as they move forward in this process.

This led to the tile system that you see below, which I believe is also consistent with the landing screen of the Android Application.

In the next image, the screen on the right shows a simple table view of all the contextual sheets that have been uploaded for their Excavation. It is here that the Admin is provisioned with the functionality to Edit the context sheets or upload associated drawings, two requirements that will be key for Fields Notes going forward.

We also have the Excavation creation screen, displayed next. Again, I applied a “Don’t make me think” approach to this, with a goal to make it as simple as possible for the user.

I broke the form up into three sections, Site Details, Location Details, and Site Members, with a clear call to action button of “Create Site” centered at the bottom of the form. The site members section allows the Admin to type in the email addresses of the users he wishes to grant access to.

Sprint 3 - Prototype & Project Closure

After I had completed the finalised version of the wireframes, work was due to begin on a low-fidelity prototype using PhotoShop and InVision. However, due to an unforeseen increased workload in my dayjob, it was instead decided at the beginning of sprint 3 that our Developer would instead create the prototype using the Bootstrap framework instead. Using the wireframes that I created in Sprint 2 as her guides, our Dev put together a full low-fidelity Bootstrap prototype.

As and when deliverables were completed, we formalised reviews and the project acceptance process with Stuart, referencing our scope document and providing him opportunity to formally sign off our work.
