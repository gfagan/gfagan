Scent - Start up Branding

Note: This section is still in progress

The Objective

To identify a potential start-up application idea and generate the necessary start-up branding content, such as a Logo and Brand Guidelines, as well as the key screens required to demo the core functionality.

Needfinding

I began by undertaking a needfinding process whereby I allowed myself a time period of around two weeks to look for any pain points that I experienced in daily living during, and from that, determined whether an application or product could be created to help resolve the matter hand.

One of the ideas that I contemplated included a musical keyboard that would help user’s learn how to play songs by providing visual feedback to the user, highlighting what keys to press in tandem with the song of the user’s choice.

Another was an application that provided for a one-stop-shop when it came to booking and paying appointments, ranging from restaurants to hairdressers. This would also allow for the automatic splitting of bills between users.

Ultimately, I started to find that the ideas that excited me most were more in the line of applications that took advantage of the camera in your phone and allowed user’s to get instant feedback about an item or service upon scanning it with their phone. This was largely inspired by the fantastic application, Vivino, which provides users with wine ratings and reviews whenever a wine label, or wine menu, is scanned. I believe the potential in this type of service is endless, and could be used to provide feedback and ratings on anything from clothes to food

But the product that appealed to me the most, and I believe would be extremely beneficial to the market, is a perfume and cologne scanner. When wandering through the airport duty free and being greeted by endless rows of fragrances, I would love to be able to simply scan the label of one or many of the bottles and instantly get some information about the product, it’s community rating, user reviews, the average cost, the type of scent that can be expected, and ability to provide my own rating or review.

Ideation

Mindmap - Name - Logo Ideas - Draft 1 - Moodboard - Finalised Logo

I decided that the name of my product would be ‘Scent’, this was largely due to the close word association involved with perfumes and colognes. I had originally narrowed it down to either that, or ‘Fragrance’, but I knew that I wanted to use the word fragrance in the description of the product, most noticeably in the banner adverts where I would inform users the Scent was a mobile fragrance scanner. In hindsight, whilst I am happy with the name, I should have taken a more expansive thinking view on this, rather than almost immediately having it down to two names in my mind and making a decision based solely on that.

I followed this up by carrying out a Mindmap excercise, which is where you write a central topic on a page and then add any related words or phrases that come to mind, expanding on each word or phrase until all possible associations are noted. When I began my mindmap, I already had decided upon the name. Ideally, the decision on the name would have waited until after this mindmapping exercise was properly completed, at which point I would, in theory, have had a wider list of associated words to choose from, and the potential for another name idea to appear in the future would be greatly diminished. Nevertheless, it was a good lesson learned.

Following on from the mindmap, I began sketching out some image associations, with much the same thought process behind the word mindmapping. Here I focused on perfume and cologne bottles, their different shapes, and how I could somehow incorporate something of this nature into my designs.

Prototyping & Initial Feedback

One of the things that constantly played on my mind when trying to decide on a logo for Scent, was the need for it to suit not only perfumes and aftershaves, but also the wide range of products and styles within these. My thought process was that I needed the keep the design somewhat neutral in terms of its color scheme and typography as a result.

I started off with a muted blue colour scheme, but quickly found this to be too bland. My desire was to achieve branding that tailors to the fragrance industry, but also on the type the company I would want Scent to be, and the image I would want to portray. With this in my mind, I ended up with arriving at the below color swatch.

The orange is a warm colour that helps bring a spark of energy to the branding. The purple tailors towards the wealthy and regal angle that many of the uppermost fragrance brands evoke. The coolness of the colour I think works very well in contrast to the orange. The green, whilst being a little vibrant, brings the notion of sophistication to the brand, and is also often associated with wealth. The red provides some passion to the branding, whilst also being muted enough to avoid dominating the colour scheme. Blue typically promotes trustworthiness, and helps provide an image that is responsible and calm. The selection of colours really helps Scent represent the extremely diverse and nuanced fragrance market, with the slightly pastel aspect of the scheme preventing from any one of the colours dominating too much and evoking the wrong image.

Once the swatch had been decided upon, I then created a scrapbook Illustrator file whereby I created multiple variations of the logo, as shown below.

My initial fear was that there was perhaps a little too much going on with the logo. One of my biggest concerns was that the ‘S’ was not prominent enough, and that the desire to incorporate the perfume spray into the composition of this was ultimately not feasible, at least not in this draft.

This was confirmed when discussing the matter with my friends, work colleagues, and team members from other Digital Skills Academy projects. The idea was nice, but the execution was not.

I went back to the drawing board, still intent on having a very visible tie to perfume or cologne in the Scent logo, and arrived at the below design.

With the reworked design I decided to stick to purple as the primary colour, and reserve the secondary colours for the faint perfume bubbles in the background. The perfume bottle I removed, instead introducing a nozzle on the of the C, and utlising the letters rounded shape in conjunction with the gestalt closure principle. A subtle touch, but with the intention being to reduce the noise of the previous design, I was far happier with this iteration.

The feedback I received from my peers, colleagues and friends this time around was far more positive, with the general impression being that this was a more professional and polished logo. However, when it came time for me to progress with the deliverables of this assignment, I found that I was running into stumbling blocks with regards to the shape, spacing and overall style of the logo. The ‘S’ being comprised of the fragrance bubbles again led to weak prominence, meaning that it couldn’t be used for standalone branding. The more I tweaked the design and thought about it’s application in both the brand guidelines and application screens for Scent, the more I distanced myself from the design.

Moodboard & Starting Afresh

I decided to go back to basics and create a moodboard using InVision’s Boards. I quickly jotted down a mindmap on paper, pulling in all the words I could think of that related to the creation of fragrances. My focus was on the core ingredients and the true essence of fragrance making.

Following this, I went on Google Image search and saved any image that took my fancy based on the keywords that the mindmap produced. Once I had these all pulled into an InVision Board, as shown below, it was clear to me that I should focus more on the ingredient, flower based angle for the Logo design, and not just the fragrance bottle and spray. I quickly built a colour palette from the images in the moodboard that I felt best represented the look and feel that I wanted associated with Scent, and then picked up my pencil and started sketching.

Finalised Logo

This process led me coming up with the idea of a rose coming out of a perfume flacon, with the stem being where the bottle’s cylinder is usually located. Once happy with my sketch, I created the finalized logo in Illustrator, which can be seen in the image below.

I opted to keep the nozzle lid incorporated into the ‘Scent’ text, but I did rework the font choice, instead this time opting for Proxima Nova. This typeface is one that lends itself very well to legibility, meaning it could be used as the main typography for Scent, but also has a nice modern feel to it, helping to promote the energetic, technology side of the Scent Brand. Purple was kept as the primary colour, helping to assimilate with the regal and affluent branding styles of many fragrances, but also being just vibrant enough to not lose sight of the modern tech feel that Scent looks to establish. Overall, I felt this Logo was a far stronger image for the company, and one that I was happy to progress with.

Brand Guidelines

As previously mentioned, it was when first attempting to create the Brand Guidelines with the old Scent Logo that I ultimately came to the conclusion that a redesign was imperative. When attempting to create an exclusion zone mapping for the logo, I realized that I had gone about things the wrong way. I created the logo, and the afterwards was checking the measurements of everything, rather than creating it first and foremost with the exclusion zones in mind. Now that I had the basis of the new logo created in Illustrator and ready to go, I set an artboard of 400px x 400px and mapped out everything in a symmetrical and even manner, which resulted in the image below.

Typography

Proxima Nova I decided to use not only as the typeface of choice for the Scent Logo, but also as the default font family for all Scent related documentation, communications, and applications. The only exception to this rule is for the iOS app, where the default typeface is SF Pro, the new system font released by Apple in iOS 11.

Proxima Nova works really well as the main typography for Scent due to the proportions, spacing, and overall look and feel, which contribute to a simple and clear typeface. With the use of open, circular forms, Proxima Nova doesn’t have a lot of fussy details or mannerisms, helping to provide a friendly appearance that is inline with the Brand Image of Scent. We want an image that is exuberant and stylish, but also isn’t so loud that it clashes with the thousands of brand images that we will encounter in the fragrance world. That is a very difficult position to balance, but Proxima Nova helps Scent massively in this regard.

Colour

Purple was chosen as the primary colour for Scent, due to its creative, imaginative and wise connotations (Miah, 2018). It’s also the colour most strongly associated with royalty, and as fragrances and perfumes were historically reserved for the aristocrats, it was the obvious choice when searching for the primary brand colour for an application like Scent. The purple chosen for Scent I believe perfectly represents the bright and creative technology nature of the brand, but also the classic and knowledgeable fragrance enthusiasts at our core. It does all this in a similar manner to our typography of choice, Proxima Nova, in that it promotes our Brand Image without dominating or clashing with the thousands of perfumes and colognes within our app.

The secondary colours shown above were derived from the moodboard consisting of the different ingredient types most commonly used in the composition of perfumes and colognes. These secondary colours are primarly reserved for placeholders such as cards, where a gradient is applied as a background to displaying a fragrance or a user’s avatar. Mobile App

I first sketched out the Wireframe using InVision’s Freehand tool, which allows you to quickly put together low-fi screens and get the general layout of the application in place. Once happy with this, I moved onto Photoshop.

The User Story for the purpose of this deliverable is for a user to identify a fragrance that they would like to know more about, take a photo of it, read the basic info and then check out some of the reviews. From this, I identified the four screens that I required:

  1. Landing Screen
  2. Photo/Scan Screen
  3. Results/Info Screen
  4. Reviews Screen

For the bottom tabs, I decided upon the following menu items.

  1. Home
  2. Search
  3. Camera
  4. Notifications
  5. Profile

Using flaticon, I downloaded the .psd file for all but the Camera icon, converted it to a white colour fill, and imported the icon into the relevant location. For the Camera icon, I decided to create the icon myself using Illustrator, due to its importance in the user story and the overall user experience of Scent. This icon needed to be extremely obvious and accessible to the user, hence it being at the center and of a greater size to the other accompanying icons.

Again, to drive user engagement, I opted for a ‘Recommended For You’ section, and also an editable ‘Your Favourite’, section, which allows users to set their Top 5 Fragrances.
